## üîê Step 5: Identified Best Practices
- Use at least **12 or more characters**
- Include **uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special symbols**
- Avoid **dictionary words or predictable patterns**
- Never reuse passwords across accounts
- Consider **passphrases** (easy to remember, hard to guess)

---

## üßæ Step 6: Tips Learned
- Mix **letters, numbers, and symbols**
- Use **different cases** (A, a)
- Avoid **personal info** like birthdays or names
- Change passwords regularly
- Use password managers to store them securely
- Example of a good password: `Sun!Rise#At7AM2025`

---

## ‚öîÔ∏è Step 7: Common Password Attacks
| Attack Type | Description | Prevention |
|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Brute Force Attack** | Tries all possible combinations until it finds a match | Use long, complex passwords |
| **Dictionary Attack** | Uses a list of common passwords or words | Avoid dictionary-based words |
| **Phishing Attack** | Tricks users into revealing passwords | Never click suspicious links |
| **Credential Stuffing** | Uses leaked passwords from other sites | Use unique passwords per account |

---

## üîé Step 8: Password Complexity and Security
The stronger and longer a password is, the harder it becomes to crack.  
- A **weak password** (like `password123`) can be broken in seconds.  
- A **complex password** (like `My$ecure#Key2025!@`) might take millions of years.  

Password complexity exponentially increases the time and computational power required for attacks.

Passphrases are practical alternatives for strong and memorable passwords.
